The Property Tax is Regressive

 

Nobody enjoys opening their property tax bill, but for lower-income folks, it’s a particularly worrisome experience.

Why? Because the less money you make, the more of it you’ll owe to your local public schools, parks and other government agencies.

Imagine seeing that the amount you owe in property taxes is more than 30% of what you earned for the entire year. That’s likely the case for Cook County homeowners making $10,000 or less, according to a Cook County Treasurer’s analysis of income tax data provided by the Illinois Department of Revenue.

Figure 1

Using that IDOR data, which did not include any individual identifying characteristics, the Treasurer’s Office calculated the average percentage of income paid by tax filers in income ranges up to $250,000. (See Appendix B in the Treasurer’s Office Property Tax Primer[HD1]  for more detail.)

If you make between $10,000 and $20,000, chances are you will pay more than 20% of your wages in property taxes. And, if you make $40,000 or less, you’ll likely pay 10% or more or your earnings in property taxes.

Want to pay 5% or less of your income on property taxes? You’ll have to earn at least $135,000 a year.

Of course, people who filed for the property tax credit get a bit of a break. Although the resulting income tax credit, as a percentage of income, is greater for lower-wage earners, it isn’t nearly enough to eliminate the regressivity of property taxes.

A regressive tax forces the poor to hand over a larger share of their earnings. �A progressive tax, like a graduated income tax, places greater burdens on those who make more money.

The property tax — which dates back to Mesopotamia — for centuries was considered progressive, because wealth was held in the form of land, and land produced income. But that changed with the industrial revolution, when wealth began to be held in many other forms: owning businesses and making investments that, in turn, generated income.

During the 20th Century, owning property became just one of many ways to build wealth. And in some cases, property ownership became disconnected from wealth. That occurred when an individual inherited a property and had a lower income than their forebears; when people spent their life paying for their home and their incomes dropped during retirement; or when the value of their property soared due to gentrification and the owner’s income did not.

The Department of Revenue data may not even capture just how regressive the property tax is, given that a greater percentage of low income residents rent their homes. �

Property taxes are built into the monthly rent people pay; they’re just not itemized. Some states recognize this and give renters income tax credits based on a percentage of their rental payments.

The property tax’s regressive nature is one aspect of many highlighted in Cook County Treasurer Maria Pappas’ Property Tax Primer[HD2] [HD3]  posted on her office’s website.

The primer explains the property tax process and delves deeply into topics that include: reasons why Illinois property taxes are so high; comparisons to property tax systems in other states; and how property tax collections are enforced through the sale of tax liens to private investors.
