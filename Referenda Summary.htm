The Few Decide

Only One in Three Registered Voters Determine the Fate of Cook County Property Tax Referendums

 

Cook County Treasurer Maria Pappas

 

 

Summary

In Illinois,  referendums are designed to give voters the final say over local governments’ power to tax and spend beyond state-imposed limits.. But low voter turnout  means that a distinct minority of voters typically make those crucial decisions. a new Cook County Treasurer’s Office analysis found.

The Treasurer’s Office examined 75 property tax-related referendums[1]  held in Cook County elections from 2020 to 2023.[2] While more than seven in 10 passed, average voter turnout was a low 32% — ranging from just 4% to 74%.[3]

Those who did vote decided whether to increase taxes, issue new debt, create or dissolve taxing districts, and grant or eliminate powers that allow their municipal governments to enact a broader array of taxes. The referendums that passed allowed local governments to:

·         Proceed with 27 bond issues that created $1.16 billion in new debt

·         Exceed limits on tax levy increases by more than $58 million

·         Increase tax levies by $5.2 million above state limits to pay off existing bond debt

·         Adopt home rule powers — which give municipal officials greater power to impose taxes, including the authority to exceed limits on property tax levy increases — in three suburbs

Referendums passed at the highest rate — more than 83% — in even-year primary elections. Primaries  tend to attract voters who are focused on national, state and partisan issues and may not be well versed on local issues or specifics of the referendums on the ballot. An average of about one in four registered voters weighed in on referendums during  primaries, when voters approved 17 of 18 proposed bond issues and tax increases, while rejecting home rule powers in three out of five cases.

The next-highest  rate at which voters approved referendums — about 68% — was in even-year general elections, in which voters pick national and state leaders. These elections draw higher turnout, with nearly half of the voters weighing in on referendums.  Voters  in these elections   might not be familiar with local issues.

Voters were least likely to back referendums in consolidated elections — with a passage rate of about 61%. — when  people are voting  solely on local issues and are likely more attuned to local politics. Less than one in five registered voters weighed in on referendums in  consolidated elections.

Whether a referendum succeeds depends on many factors, including turnout, the nature of the question posed, the ability of referendum supporters  to get their voters to the polls and where the  question is placed on the ballot. Those factors limit the ability to draw broad conclusions about referendums and voters .

Still, it is clear that a minority of all registered voters  decides a referendum’s fate  Only 12% of referendums  in the past four years drew more than 50% of voters to the polls, while 47% of the referendums saw less than 25% of voters weigh in.

That may be something for people  to ponder, given that at the time of this analysis, the March 2024 primary election is near. Suburban Chicago  voters  are to consider  13 referendums that allow five bond issues totaling $152.3 million, four tax rate increases that  exceed limits by a total of more than $8.6 million,  two smaller suburbs to  adopt powers that give them more taxing authority, creation of a new mental health district with taxing powers and one to dissolve a northwest suburban fire protection district that stopped operating in October 2023.

In addition, Chicago is asking voters to decide whether to enact significantly higher real estate transfer taxes[4] on properties that sell for $1 million, while lowering taxes on properties that sell for less.

Types of Referendums that Impact  Property Taxes

Issue Property Tax-Backed Debt Above State Limits

To issue general obligation bonds — which are backed by property taxes  — that exceed  borrowing limits, school districts, non-home rule cities and villages, park districts and other local governments are required to seek voter approval through referendums.[5] Bonds are a form of long-term borrowing, akin to a home’s mortgage, typically used by governments to pay for major projects with long-term benefits that cannot be paid for with existing revenues.

During the time period analyzed, 34 referendums sought  a total of $1.3 billion in general obligation debt authorization. Of those, 27 passed  for a success rate  of 79%. As a result, local governments issued $1.16 billion in bonds.

Increase Limiting Rate

The Property Tax Extension Limitation Law, known by its acronym PTELL, limits how much government officials can raise taxes. PTELL restricts the overall amount of property taxes levied by non-home rule taxing districts to no more than the prior year’s increase in the Consumer Price Index, or 5%, whichever is less.[6] Government officials can tax more than that if  voters agree.

During the period studied, governments offered 15 referendums  to exceed the levy caps. Of those, 11 passed — a passing rate of 73%. As a result, levy caps were exceeded by a total of $58 million in those 11 taxing districts.

Exceed Limits for Increasing Property Taxes for non-Referendum Bond Payments

Non-home rule governments may issue general obligation bonds without seeking voter approval based on the value of district’s taxable property.[7] Other types of bonds — including those used to refund other bonds, create a working cash fund, and improve water or wastewater treatment facilities — don’t require referendums.[8] Under PTELL, taxes levied to pay this non-referendum bond debt is limited to what’s known as a Debt Service Extension Base. Annual increases to that base, like other annual property tax increases, is limited to 5% or the increase in the consumer price index, whichever is less. To exceed those limitations, officials seek voter approval.

During the period analyzed, two school districts  sought to increase tax levies by a total of $5.2 million to pay bond debt. In the 2022 primary , Lindop School District 92 in Broadview asked voters to establish a debt extension base at $425,000, which would allow for $6 million in new bonds for school repairs. Later that year, Lemont High School District 210 asked voters in the general election to increase the debt service extension base from $249,601 to $5 million, an increase of $4.75 million. Approve or Eliminate Home Rule Powers

Cities and villages with populations of 25,000 or more automatically possess home rule powers, which allow them to enact an array of taxes, issue additional debt and exceed property tax limitations. Less populace  municipalities can possess those same powers if voters approve, And, those powers can also be taken away  through referendums.

During the period analyzed,  10 municipalities asked voters for  home rule powers. The populations of Harvey and Melrose Park dropped  below 25,000, so both municipalities were required to ask voters in 2022 if they should lose their home rule status.[9] Voters  in Harvey and Melrose Park said they should keep their  home rule taxing powers.

The other eight sought home rule powers. Out of those, voters in Barrington, Hometown, and Matteson agreed to give their government leaders new powers Hometown’s home rule referendum passed by only two votes, the closest vote of any referendum during the period studied.

Create or Dissolve Taxing Districts

In order to create or  to get rid of a taxing district,  say a park district or fire protection district, a majority of  voters must approve . Creating a new taxing district typically results in new property taxes.

The village of University Park, sought  to create a park district. Village leaders v asked voters to approve the district with an annual tax levy no greater than $800,000. It  failed because only 50% voted yes in  Cook and Will counties. In Illinois, a majority of voters must approve in order to create a park district.[10]

Governments also can create boards to raise taxes   to provide mental health care services. Governments in the suburbs must get voter approval to establish mental health boards and to levy an additional tax no greater than 0.15%.[11] Such a  referendum may be initiated by government officials through a resolution or by citizens through a petition.[12]

Three Cook County townships asked voters to agree to new taxes  to provide community mental health facilities and services . Voters in Schaumburg and Wheeling townships approved their referendums  and voters in Thornton Township defeated the proposal.

However, the results were challenged due to concerns  the referendums’ language did not meet state requirements and include the true monetary impact of the tax. In fall 2023, state lawmakers subsequently allowed the townships to levy the tax without going back to voters with a new referendum. As of early 2024, officials in Wheeling Township remain concerned over legal challenges and have yet to agree on imposing the new tax on property owners , while Schaumburg Township is moving ahead with the new taxes.

In Chicago, Expanded Mental Health Services Programs can be created within community areas. These programs expand mental health services for a designated territory[13] by levying an additional property tax rate of 0.025% to 0.044%. Voters must approve these programs  in a governmental referendum  or by a citizen petition.[14] Voters are asked to approve two separate questions — one to approve the tax rate[15] and the other to establish the maximum levy.[16]

During the period analyzed, 11 community areas voted to establish three Expanded Mental Health Programs. In Chicago,  political coalitions for years have stressed the need for additional mental health services. All six Chicago referendums passed by overwhelming margins of 76% or more.

Enact or Increase a Real Estate Transfer Tax

A real estate transfer tax imposes a tax on  property sales equal to a percentage of the sale price. If government officials want to enact or increase the real estate transfer tax, they must get voters’ support.[17]

Change to an existing real estate transfer tax was up for a vote once during the period analyzed. Oak Lawn officials in 2022 asked voters to close a loophole in the  existing real estate transfer tax. Commercial owners avoided paying the tax  through transfer of controlling interests of real estate, like the sale of shares of stock in a corporation. The referendum failed with 70% of voters voting against it.

Results and Findings

Voter turnout for the referendums  averaged  32%. The 2020 Presidential race  was the only election where  more than half of registered voters went to the polls.

Average turnout for property tax referendums in the six elections analyzed fell just under 32%. Turnout was even lower for consolidated and primary elections, with average turnouts of 19% and 26%.

The April 2021 consolidated election had the lowest average turnout, when just 15% of those registered cast a vote in seven referendums. Three passed, including support for  bond issues for the Glencoe Park District, with just 12.5% of voters participating, and the northwest suburban Palatine Rural Fire Protection District, where only  5.6% of registered voters decided the outcome.

General elections, on the other hand, saw the highest turnout for referendums. The 2020 presidential election had an average referendum turnout of 64% — the only election where more than 50% of registered voters decide a referendum’s fate.

Low turnout means a minority of taxpayers are making decisions on debt and tax increases.

Voters in most cases are failing to turn out for questions designed to ensure local taxpayers have a say in how schools, libraries, parks, fire districts and other local governments incur debt or increase annual tax levies above state-imposed limits. On average, less than a third of registered voters in Cook County cast a vote for issuing bonds, increasing taxes to pay for non-referendum debt, or exceeding tax levy caps.

In the most extreme example, only 4% of registered voters in the Long Grove Fire Protection District approved a $16.5 million bond that helped finance a new fire station, the lowest turnout for any referendums during the four-year period.  Although there were more than 18,000 registered voters , only 798.

Low turnout means a few votes decide the outcome. In the 2022 primary election, Arlington Heights School District 25 asked voters to approve a $75 million bond to repair school buildings and to construct additional classrooms for full-day kindergarten. With 28% of those registered actually  voting, the referendum passed by less than 0.5 percentage points — one of the lowest margins for of referendums analyzed.

Not all referendums  lead to higher taxes

Although a majority of referendum sought tax increases, some led to savings. Two referendums in 2020 asked voters to dissolve various departments and taxing districts, in part to lower government costs.

Five taxing districts said  approval of their referendums would not mean  higher property tax bills because other existing government debt was expiring. What officials did not  emphasize is that without the new tax measures, property owners would see even greater decreases in their taxes. All five referendums passed.

That was the case for the county’s largest bond approved during the period analyzed. In the April 2023 consolidated election, voters authorized  $179 million in bonds to be issued for Elgin School District 46 by a margin of more than 25 percentage points — with just 13% of voters casting a vote.

That additional debt, which officials said would be used to rebuild five elementary schools, did not increase the school district’s tax levy, as the new bond replaces retiring debt. However, if voters rejected the proposal, the district’s annual debt service levy would have dropped from about $30 million to $20 million in 2024 and between $15 to $17 million from 2025-2032.

Local officials made similar arguments  when they asked voters to exceed tax limits for non-referendum bond debt under PTELL. Both Lindop School District 92 and Lemont High School District 210 assured voters increasing the debt service extension base would have zero impact on their tax burden.

Instead, the increases would allow the school districts to maintain the amount of debt payments once current debt is paid off. The district would then use the increased debt capacity to fund additional projects. However, similar to the bond approvals, the tax burden would eventually decrease if voters rejected the referendums.

One thing is clear: most Cook County referendums are decided by a few  voters

Referendums were more likely to pass during primaries. The combination of low turnout, the focus of voters on state and federal issues and a motivated  group of voters supporting the referendums might explain why referendums were more likely to pass during primaries.

Other factors also play a role in whether a referendum succeeds or fails, which makes each unique, that it’s difficult to draw  general conclusions. What is clear is that low voter turnout a minority of highly engaged voters are making these decisions for their communities.
